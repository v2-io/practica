# Cluster 05 — Failure-mode defaults

What the system structurally resists. The claims in this cluster shape decisions about Practica's *defaults* — what the system makes easier and what it makes harder — with explicit attention to known failure modes. The architectural commitments (cluster 01) and coordination affordances (cluster 02) define the system's capacity; the defaults here shape its *behavior under stress*, where instinct typically reaches for the wrong move.

The claims here rest on (a) paper 1's identification of bootstrap-recovery as a forced consequence of serial context-turnover; (b) the harvest's *triple-grounded* convergences (H1+O3+SC4 on long-confident efforts; H4+SS4+E5 on DAG-depth fragility); (c) Bungay's military-doctrine identification of the toxic-cycle anti-pattern and the centralization-under-stress failure mode; (d) the 1888 Field Service Regulations' canonical "failure to act > mistake of means."

---

## F1 — Bootstrap-recovery-safety

**Practica's plumbing-layer state is readable and restorable by external means (filesystem inspection, plain-text reading, manual editing) without requiring its own intelligence layer to be operational. The recovery path is *substrate-level* (filesystem, plain text, version control), not *application-level* (the running tool).**

Why this is forced: under the structural identification from paper 1 (plumbing/intelligence split), the plumbing layer is *the* artifact of structural continuity. When the intelligence layer fails — bug, context-turnover, intentional shutdown, deliberate refusal — the plumbing-layer state is the only artifact of the system's commitments that survives. Under serial context-turnover (which characterizes Practica's deployment), intelligence-layer failure is the *expected* case at the end of every session, not an edge case. The plumbing layer mediates every handoff. If it requires the prior sub-agent's runtime to be inspectable, the handoff fails by design. Operata named this explicitly in its TODO: *"If a bug in operata prevents you from reading your task list, you can't see what to fix. Need a stable/unstable separation."* Operata also lived the failure mode — the *"accidentally wiped"* prior session state recorded in its Lost State section.[^f1-source]

What this does not specify: the specific durability substrate (SQLite, append-only log, content-addressed objects, git-tracked plain text — all compatible); the schema; the recovery protocols. The commitment is to *substrate-level recoverability without dependence on the intelligence layer*.

*Tier:* Convergent — paper 1's entailment trace + operata's lived engineering catastrophe. Cross-references: A1 in [[01-architectural-commitments]] (the plumbing layer is what must remain recoverable); C1 in [[02-coordination-affordances]] (soft-claiming requires the next agent to be able to read the stale claim signal — bootstrap-recovery is the precondition); F5 below (anti-centralization-under-stress includes bootstrap-recovery as a structural enabler — the system must be inspectable when stress instinct demands centralized intervention).

## F2 — Force re-examination of long-confident efforts

**Practica's defaults *force re-examination* of efforts that have been confidently-tracked for a long time, on a schedule that depends on confidence-accumulation rate rather than only on uncertainty signals. The system surfaces long-confident efforts as candidates for explicit re-checking — not because they look failing, but because they have been confident for long enough to be at structural risk.**

Why this is forced: this is the harvest's strongest *triple-grounded* convergence — three independent AAT mechanisms point at the same operational imperative. (a) **H1 — stability-induced myopia.** Under a Beta-Bernoulli edge-credence whose sector parameter scales as $\sim 1/(n+1)$, an agent without a forgetting mechanism gets monotonically slower at noticing a regime change. Confidence-accumulation reduces the rate at which contradicting evidence can shift belief. (b) **O3 — Lyapunov-survival drive.** A confident agent in a drifting world is *forced* to explore for pristine, low-noise observations to penetrate its own stubbornness — exploration is required when uncertainty is *low* and the world is drifting, not just when uncertainty is *high*. (c) **SC4 — adversarial targeting concentrates where the defender's response is slowest.** Long-trusted dependencies and high-confidence regions are structurally the attack surface; cybersecurity incidents cluster on long-trusted dependencies, and adversarial-ML examples cluster on high-confidence model regions. Three independent mechanisms — self-coherence (H1), drift-defense (O3), adversarial-defense (SC4) — converge on the *same* design imperative: *force periodic re-examination of long-trusted efforts.*[^f2-source]

This is one of the strongest convergence-grounded design directives in the harvest. The instinct to *not* re-examine a confident effort (*"why fix what isn't broken"*) is exactly what each of the three mechanisms predicts will fail. Practica must structurally *invite* the re-examination — perhaps as a queue of *"long-confident efforts worth a fresh look"* rather than as alerts about specific failures.

What this does not specify: the re-examination protocol (full re-derivation? backbrief refresh? targeted probe?); the schedule (calendar-based? confidence-accumulation-based? both?); how *"confidence"* is operationalized in a way that the schedule can act on it. The commitment is to the *structural forcing of re-examination*; the specific affordance has room.

*Tier:* Convergent — triple-grounded within AAT (H1 robust-qualitative + O3 home-verify-pending + SC4 cross-segment structural argument). Each individual mechanism would be hypothesis-grade; the convergence elevates the operational directive. Cross-references: G1 in [[04-diagnostic-surfaces]] (per-direction monitoring is one of the signals that surfaces long-confident efforts deserving re-examination); F5 below (anti-centralization-under-stress works against the *"don't disturb what's working"* instinct that prevents re-examination); D5 in [[03-content-discipline]] (COG-as-recurring-attending is one specific form of forced re-examination for the most foundational content).

## F3 — Failure-to-act-vs-mistake-of-means: defaults that do not penalize action

**Practica's defaults never make blame-assignment easier than learning-surfacing. Long-running unresolved efforts are visible as worse than rapid wrong actions visibly corrected. The 1888 Prussian Field Service Regulations' canonical statement — *"a failure to act or a delay is a more serious fault than making a mistake in the choice of means"* — is implemented as a structural design commitment, not as a stylistic preference.**

Why this is forced: Bungay Ch 6 cites the 1888 FSR and a Moltke acolyte's account: *"you will extinguish a hundred positive initiatives in order to prevent one error, and thereby lose a tremendous amount of energy."* The 100:1 ratio is the structural claim, not a normative aspiration. Many existing tracking tools (post-mortems, root-cause dashboards, *"owner"* fields highlighting failure, blame-attribution workflows) optimize for *assignability of failure* — structurally exactly the wrong instinct given the 100:1 trade-off. Practica's defaults must not reproduce this: the *post-attempt* surface foregrounds what was learned, not who fell short. The C5 *refuse-with-reason* operation (cluster 02) is part of this commitment — a structural action with reasoning is preferred to a structural delay or omission, even if the action turns out to be wrong.[^f3-source]

What this does not specify: the specific surfaces that foreground learning (post-mortem templates? per-effort *what-we-learned* fields? structural acknowledgment of mistakes-as-information?); the absence-detection that surfaces *delays* (when does the absence-of-action become structurally legible?); the interaction with accountability (foregrounding learning does not mean removing accountability — it means accountability is for the *learning loop*, not for the absence of error). The commitment is to the *defaults that do not penalize action* and to the *long-running unresolved* category being structurally surfaced as worse than rapid-wrong-visibly-corrected.

*Tier:* Bungay's military-doctrine identification with engineering-relevant operationalization. The harvest's S4 entry treats this as a tool-defaults principle. Cross-references: C5 in [[02-coordination-affordances]] (refuse-with-reason is the structured form of *"action with reasoning preferred to silent delay"*); D2 in [[03-content-discipline]] (70%-right intent commits are the intent-side analogue — incomplete intent committed is preferred to delayed-perfect intent uncommitted); F4 below (the toxic-cycle's *"more controls"* instinct is partly driven by the same blame-assignment optimization F3 names).

[^f3-source]: Bungay Ch 6 (`~/src/_ref/books/Art-of-Action/parts/06-the-effects-gap.md`) citing 1888 Prussian Field Service Regulations: *"you will extinguish a hundred positive initiatives in order to prevent one error, and thereby lose a tremendous amount of energy."* The harvest's S4 entry: *"Failure to act is a graver fault than mistake in choice of means; one punished good-faith mistake costs ~100 future initiatives. … Many existing tracking tools (post-mortems, root-cause dashboards, 'owner' fields highlighting failure) optimize for assignability of failure — structurally exactly the wrong instinct at the 100:1 trade-off."*

## F4 — Resist the toxic-cycle triple

**Practica's defaults resist the three intuitive-but-toxic responses the alignment-autonomy framework identifies: (1) *more detailed information* in response to knowledge gaps (data-lake / dashboard sprawl); (2) *more detailed instructions* in response to alignment gaps (task-template proliferation); (3) *more detailed controls* in response to effects gaps (input-metric scoreboards). Each individual move can be locally OK; their *combination* is the disease.**

Why this is forced: Bungay Ch 2 develops the three-gap formalism — Knowledge / Alignment / Effects gaps emerge from friction; the *intuitive* response to each is precisely wrong; and *the three responses combined* in one organization are the toxic cycle that produced the canonical case studies. Bungay's framing rules out the autonomy/centralization axis as the diagnosis (devolved-tech and centralized-pharma exhibit the same disease across opposite cultures) — the failure is not under-control or over-control, it is the *toxic-cycle triple* operating together. For Practica, this is a defaults claim: the tool's natural growth direction (toward more fields, more states, more metrics, more templates, more dashboards) is the wrong direction. Defaults must favor subtraction over addition. Specific defaults to resist: the *data-lake-as-knowledge-tool* default (no dashboard sprawl); the *task-template proliferation* default (no growing collection of pre-fabricated task shapes); the *input-metric scoreboard* default (no leaderboards on activity-metrics).[^f4-source]

What this does not specify: whether any individual move from the triple is *forbidden* (no — the toxic cycle is the *combination*); how subtraction defaults are operationalized (deletion suggestions? capacity caps? archival defaults?); the boundary between *useful* and *toxic-cycle* growth. The commitment is to *defaults that resist the triple as a combined pattern*, with the recognition that any of the three in isolation can be locally appropriate.

*Tier:* Bungay's military-doctrine identification (Ch 2, the three-gap formalism and the toxic-cycle triple). The harvest's S2 entry treats this as a foundational anti-pattern claim. Cross-references: D1 in [[03-content-discipline]] (the minimum-sufficient-set discipline is the intent-side defense against the *"more detailed information"* limb of the toxic cycle); F5 below (anti-centralization-under-stress is the temporal analogue — the toxic-cycle triple intensifies under stress); F3 above (the *"more detailed controls"* limb is partly driven by the blame-assignment optimization F3 names).

[^f4-source]: Bungay Ch 2 (`~/src/_ref/books/Art-of-Action/parts/02-the-cause.md`) Figs. 4–5 — Knowledge / Alignment / Effects gaps and the intuitive-response triple. The harvest's S2 entry: *"each AAT-derived directive and each operata constraint maps to one or more gaps; the three-gap structure may be the natural organization for the distilled 02. Strong direct UX-defaults implication: practica must not, by default, enact any of the three intuitive responses — no data-lake / dashboard sprawl as the response to uncertainty; no task-template proliferation as the response to mis-execution; no input-metric scoreboards as the response to disappointing results. Each individual move can be locally OK; their combination is the disease."*

## F5 — Anti-centralization-under-stress

**Practica's defaults make over-specification *harder* under stress, not easier. The tool surfaces mechanisms that *resist* senior-overrule of sub-agent decisions, surfaces costs of intervention, pulls the user's attention back to the higher intent rather than into operational details. The instinct to take more control under stress — Bungay's identified Hitler-as-failure-mode — is exactly the instinct the tool must structurally resist.**

Why this is forced: Bungay Ch 3 develops the canonical failure mode: *"Auftragstaktik is not popular with tyrants."* When Hitler intervened more in the Eastern Front from 1942 onward, success faltered; centralization-under-stress accelerated the German Army's failure precisely because it removed the mechanism that had been compensating for chaos. The pattern is general: the instinct under stress is to take more control, but that instinct reasserts exactly the toxic-cycle responses F4 names — more information, more instructions, more controls. *The tool will be most needed when things are going badly, and that is exactly the moment when users will be tempted to over-specify.* The defaults must structurally resist this. This is uncomfortable design — it asks the tool to be helpful by being *mildly resistant* to certain instincts that feel correct at the moment.[^f5-source]

What this does not specify: the specific resistance mechanisms (cost-surfacing? confirmation prompts? structural friction? alternate paths?); the threshold at which resistance kicks in (stress detected by what signal?); how the tool distinguishes legitimate cascade-up from anti-pattern centralization (this is where C3's three-state backbrief and C2's two-levels-up visibility are load-bearing — legitimate cascade-up via backbrief is different from override-from-above). The commitment is to *defaults that make over-specification harder under stress* rather than easier.

*Tier:* Bungay's military-doctrine identification (Ch 3, the Hitler-as-failure-mode case) with engineering-relevant defaults operationalization. Cross-references: F4 above (anti-centralization-under-stress is the temporal pattern; the toxic-cycle triple F4 names is what centralization-under-stress reaches for); C2 in [[02-coordination-affordances]] (two-levels-up visibility supports the legitimate cascade-up; anti-pattern centralization is the opposite — it pushes operational detail *down* from the top); D4 in [[03-content-discipline]] (the command/control separation is structurally relevant — anti-centralization defends the command layer from being driven by control-side panic).

[^f5-source]: Bungay Ch 3 (`~/src/_ref/books/Art-of-Action/parts/03-elements-of-a-solution.md`) develops the Hitler-as-failure-mode case. Working paper synthesis at `~/src/practica/msc/s3-working/03-elements-of-a-solution.md` §1.17 and §3.F: *"Centralization-under-stress as the structural failure mode. … When Hitler intervened more, success faltered. The instinct under stress is to take more control — this is exactly the instinctive response from Ch 2 Fig 5 (more detailed information / instructions / controls), reasserting itself precisely when the system is most disturbed."*

## F6 — DAG-depth fragility warning at design time

**Practica's UX surfaces a warning when an effort DAG's chain depth approaches conditions where deep chains become structurally fragile, and proactively suggests instrumentation or restructuring options. The warning is *triple-grounded*: operational (depth-fragility is empirically observed in software-engineering chains); structural (deep edges are less-tested because gated by the product of upstream success rates); axiomatic (chain-confidence-decay is forced by the chain-rule of probability via Cauchy-FE additivity).**

Why this is forced: the harvest's H4 entry (operational depth-fragility) and SS4 entry (axiomatic coordinate-forcing grounding) plus E5 (operata's engineering recognition that DAG-with-cycle-detection was needed from day one but was unshipped at abandonment) form a *triple-grounded* convergence at three different epistemic levels. **H4** composes three penalties on plan depth: multiplicative chain-confidence decay; evidence starvation (the deepest node is the *least*-tested because its edge is only exercised when all upstream succeed); information cost of holding the strategy in working memory. **SS4** sharpens this with an axiomatic grounding: chain-confidence-decay is forced by the chain-rule of probability via Cauchy-FE additivity (Aczél 1966). *Deep chains are exponentially fragile as a structural fact about probability itself, not as a contingent property of the AAT machinery.* **E5** is the engineering-side witness: operata recognized DAG-with-cycle-detection was forced (recovered in A4 in [[01-architectural-commitments]]) but didn't have the depth-fragility warning surface — depth was visible in the data but not flagged at design time.[^f6-source]

What this does not specify: the depth threshold (this is tier-dependent and may need verification at the home segments before driving a specific design constant; the qualitative claim holds at synthesis tier); the form of the warning (visual indicator? blocking confirmation? suggestion? log entry?); the suggested restructuring options (parallelize? shorten chain? add instrumentation?); how depth interacts with other dimensions of fragility (cross-cutting edges may modulate the chain-rule penalty). The commitment is to *surfacing depth as a design-time concern*, with the qualitative claim (deep chains are fragile and the deepest step is the one you've tested least) carried robustly.

*Tier:* Convergent — operational (H4) + structural (chain-rule additivity from probability theory) + engineering precedent (operata recognized DAG structure as theorem-forced via SS1's `def-strategy-dag`). Cross-references: A4 in [[01-architectural-commitments]] (DAG-with-cycle-detection from day one — F6 is the runtime/design-time complement to A4's structural commitment); G1 in [[04-diagnostic-surfaces]] (per-direction monitoring may need to track depth-of-dependency as a dimension); F2 above (deep chains often become long-confident efforts because their deepest nodes are rarely exercised — F2 and F6 reinforce each other).

[^f6-source]: Harvest H4 (`~/src/agentic-systems/01-aat-core/src/impl-strategy-dynamics.md`); Harvest SS4 (`~/src/agentic-systems/01-aat-core/src/impl-strategy-structure.md` + `~/src/agentic-systems/01-aat-core/src/disc-additive-coordinate-forcing.md` — the four-layer Cauchy-FE / Hobson / Aczél / Čencov axiomatic grounding). The harvest SS4 entry: *"This sharpens H4 (deep DAG fragility) — depth-fragility is not contingent on edge-rate; it's forced by the chain-rule of probability via Cauchy-FE. ## What this cluster does not specify

The failure-mode defaults here are *what the system structurally resists*. They are silent on:

- **The architectural substrate the defaults operate on.** The plumbing/intelligence split, type-separated resources, DAG-with-cycle-detection — these are in [[01-architectural-commitments]].
- **The operations the defaults shape.** Soft-claiming, backbrief, refuse-with-reason — these are in [[02-coordination-affordances]]; the defaults here shape *which paths through the operations are easier or harder*, not *what the operations are*.
- **The content the defaults govern.** Minimum-sufficient-set, 70%-right commits, the toxic-cycle anti-patterns at the content level — these are in [[03-content-discipline]]; F4 in this cluster is the *defaults* counterpart of the content discipline.
- **The diagnostic signals the defaults consume.** Per-direction monitoring, long-confidence flagging, regime classification — these are in [[04-diagnostic-surfaces]]; F2 and F6 use these signals to decide when re-examination / warnings are appropriate.
- **What the defaults cannot promise.** Cultural-soil readiness for the discipline; the moral-hazard limit; the sandbox-to-deployment transport — these honest limits are in [[06-limits-and-positioning]].

The defaults are the *behavioral shape* of the system under stress and over time. They are where the architectural commitments and content disciplines meet the actual conditions Practica is deployed into; they shape what happens when the user reaches for an instinct that the framework predicts is wrong.
